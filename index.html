Finance

1. Algorithmic Trading

The following is a backtest of my mean-reversion algorithm from Quantopian. It rebelances weekly, longing previous week worst preformed (bottom 10%) of liquid stocks and shorting best preformed stocks. During the backtest period: 2015-03 to 2016-04, it consistently outperformed benchmark, which signifies that mean-reversion algorithm does pariticularly well during market downtime, and has low beta and low volatility. 

#liquid securities 
low_returns = recent_returns.percentile_between(0,10,mask=high_dollar_volume)
high_returns = recent_returns.percentile_between(90,100,mask=high_dollar_volume)

#For each security in the universe, order long or short positions with equal weights
    if data.can_trade(stock):
        if stock in context.long_secs.index:
            order_target_percent(stock, context.long_weight)
        elif stock in context.short_secs.index:
            order_target_percent(stock, context.short_weight)

Return-to-Mean algorithm relies on the assumption that the market is super efficient; that is all fundamental and risk factors are already spelled out in the price, hence return is nothing but a random variable. On the other hand, an opposite thought, momentums, believes that market is a mixture of sensitments and it is operated by irretional human beings: higher high and lower low.

Despite that we all conveniently agree that "the simpler, the better", we are dying to do a little better; I have learned many advanced techniques and tried to incorporate them into the simple mean-reversion and momemtum algo: (check them out in my other projects categories.)

► Mechine Learning: PsychSignal's stockTwits trader mood, run Twitter feed in Alchemy api (NLP), chart/data pattern recognition & categorization (supervised/unsupervised learning).

► Numerical Method: modern portfolio theory, dynamic mode decomposition (PCA), random matrix theory.

► Web/App design: experienced using Python, C++, C#, R, SQL, and JavaScript, I built high-quality financial apps for porfolio manager and traders to automate their works.

2. Paper-Money Option Trading

While learning portfolio theory with a goal in meeting practice, I created trading algorithms; while learning derivatives, I traded paperMoney options from TD Ameritrade.

As of 5/25/16, after a week of straight breakout descending triangle, AAPL is on the rise. The 100 call monthlys at the money is traded at $1.76, expiring in 3 weeks' time. One can also sell in the money call option to make a bull spread taking advantage of the low VIX environment.

3. Computational Finance

With deep understanding of numerical analysis(schemas, monto carlo, bootstrap), PDEs(stochastics, fourier), and probability(martingale, limiting theorem), I wrote computation algorthms to meet both accuracy and speed.

I'm also familiar with many popular computational toolboxes and libraries. In the graph below, I used Plotly to make an interactive graph. 

library(plotly)
p <- plot_ly(economics, x = date, y = 100*unemploy / pop, name = "unemply rate") %>% 
      add_trace(y = fitted(loess(100*unemploy / pop ~ as.numeric(date))),
                x = date, name="trend") %>%
      layout(title="Unemployment")
